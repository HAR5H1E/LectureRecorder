# The Scientific Method: Evaluating the Flat Earth Model

## Introduction
This paper aims to examine the predictions made by the flat earth model. Specifically, it aims to document the results of observations made to test whether these predictions are accurate. The observations focus on:
* Whether sunlight can be seen illuminating plants in different locations.
* Whether the Southern Cross constellation can be observed at night.

## The Flat Earth Model
The model makes assumptions about how sunlight and other celestial objects should behave with respect to an observer on Earth. 
* The model states that the sun moves above a flat surface and never approaches or passes below the horizon.
* The sun is visible above the Earth's surface at all times.
* The model predicts that all constellations should be observable from any location on Earth.

## Methodology
The model was used to generate predictions about how sunlight and other celestial objects should appear to an observer on Earth. These predictions were tested through direct observation:
* Sunrise and sunset were observed from a fixed ground-level location.
* Observations of sunlight near the horizon were conducted.
* Nighttime observations were conducted to examine the visibility of constellations from the same location.

## Observations and Results
### Sunlight and Horizon
* Observations of sunrise and sunset show that the sun was visible, and the horizon could still be seen even when the sun itself was below the horizon.
* The flat earth model predicts that sunlight should not be visible from below the horizon during sunrise.
* The model fails to account for the behavior of light and the position of the sun as it passes through the Earth's atmosphere. Atmospheric scattering and refraction allow sun rays to be visible.

### Constellations
* The Southern Cross constellation was not visible from the observation location.
* The flat earth model predicts that all constellations should be visible from any location on Earth.

## Conclusion
The results show that the flat earth model fails to provide an accurate depiction of the observed world. The model’s predictions regarding the behavior of sunlight during sunrise and sunset and the visibility of constellations were in contradiction with direct observation. Six predictions derived from the flat earth model consistently failed to match observations. The findings of this study support the conclusion that the Earth is not flat and that alternative models are required to accurately explain observed natural phenomena.
